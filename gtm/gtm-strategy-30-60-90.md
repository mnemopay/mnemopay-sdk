## PART 3: MICRONICHE DEPLOYMENT STRATEGY — GTM ACROSS ALL 13 NICHES

### The Core Finding: One Wedge, Then Sequential Expansion

Every infrastructure company that won — Stripe, Plaid, Vanta, Twilio — used the same playbook: **pick one niche, go so deep that you become synonymous with the problem, then use that as a trust bridge to the next niche.**

Vanta launched as "SOC2 automation for Series A startups" — that's it. No HIPAA, no ISO 27001, no enterprise. Once they owned that sentence, expansion was easier because enterprise buyers trusted the brand. Plaid started with small VC-backed fintechs (Venmo, Robinhood) before touching banks. Stripe served indie developers before Fortune 500.

Trying to serve 13 niches simultaneously at zero budget guarantees you own none of them. The #1 mistake founders make is using their surface area as a proxy for strength — 13 landing pages, 13 "use cases," 13 half-filled top-of-funnels, zero revenue.

---

### PLG vs. Enterprise Sales: The Answer for Your Stack

**For MnemoPay:** Hybrid, but start with **Product-Led Sales (PLS)**, not pure PLG.

Pure PLG requires a product so frictionless that buyers self-serve to revenue. MnemoPay's ACV is unknown yet but compliance infrastructure for enterprises (EU AI Act, HIPAA) skews $10K–$100K/year — above the pure PLG threshold. However, your SDK is already developer-facing, which means you can use the product as the top-of-funnel (PLG motion) and close with human-assisted sales when a user hits the enterprise tier.

**For GridStamp:** Direct outbound enterprise sales from day one. Drone delivery operators, oil/gas inspection companies, and AV fleets do not self-serve $50K compliance contracts. Their procurement cycles require a champion inside the company, a proof-of-concept, and a SOW. Use PLG only to get a POC started (free tier + quick integration).

### How Stripe/Plaid/Vanta Actually Sequenced

**Stripe:** Launched 2010 with one product: charge a card in 7 lines of code. Dominated developer payments (indie devs, YC startups) for 3 years before touching enterprise. Expanded to billing (Stripe Billing) only after payments was so dominant no competitor could displace them. Each product expansion used existing customer data + trust, not new customer acquisition.

**Plaid:** Started with Venmo, Robinhood, Coinbase — small, fast-moving fintechs who couldn't afford bank integrations. Won 8,000 fintech apps before approaching JPMorgan. The bank clients came inbound because Plaid already owned their customers' banking data flows.

**Vanta:** Launched SOC2 automation for "Series A SaaS startups who need enterprise customers." That sentence. For 2 years. 40+ competitors emerged, but Vanta's brand association was so strong with that exact problem that they won anyway. Then added HIPAA, ISO 27001, GDPR as upsells to the same customer base.

**Your sequencing should mirror Vanta's:** EU AI Act compliance (the time-pressure wedge) → then upsell same customers on AI agent insurance underwriting (Klaimee partnership) → then healthcare RCM/HIPAA (same compliance buyers, different regulation). The customer is a GRC/DevOps lead at a mid-market SaaS company. You are selling them the same product with different regulatory labels.

### The #1 Mistake to Avoid

**Naming all 13 niches on your homepage.** The moment a GRC lead at a German SaaS company sees "also used for drone delivery, cold chain pharma, and sidewalk robots" — they immediately assume you're a horizontal tool that doesn't understand their specific regulatory context. They leave.

The solution: one domain per audience (or at minimum, one landing page per ICP with no cross-contamination), and separate outreach sequences per niche. MnemoPay's homepage should say "EU AI Act compliance for AI agent developers" until you hit $50K MRR. GridStamp's homepage should say "proof-of-presence for drone delivery operators" until you have 3 paying customers in that vertical. Then you add the next.
